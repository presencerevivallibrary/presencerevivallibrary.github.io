Sister Mary, one of the most frightening accounts in the whole New Testament is what happened to Ananias and Sapphira and the way they died. What makes this so scary is that you and I know of ministries guilty of exactly the same things, yet they seem to have suffered no particular judgment for their actions.

And if that is not enough, sometimes we think only preachers are accountable enough to be judged that way. Yet Ananias and Sapphira were not exactly preachers. They were part of the congregation itself.

In fact, the verses that carry the account of what happened to Ananias and Sapphira are probably one of the worst nightmares for teachers who love to teach the books of the Bible verse by verse. And for those who love to teach the Bible by subjects, Ananias and Sapphira is a subject often avoided.

Like most preachers, I have an excellent set of Greek books to access the original language. Unfortunately, sometimes the Greek tells the story just as generically as the King James does and gives no particular insight as to why judgment would come on Ananias and Sapphira so severely over something most preachers would esteem trivial or insignificant, such as lying in some services.

Most commentators console themselves with words like "special acts of God," not knowing how to reconcile what happened to Ananias and Sapphira with the morals of the modern-day church. The general conclusion of most commentaries is that they were set forth as some kind of special example, a sort of one-time judgment.

But if this is the case, what have we learned? Greed among ministers is at an all-time high. There seems to be no fear among a growing number of preachers who represent the gospel. They seem to think the sheep's whole lives rise or fall according to what they are giving into their ministries, and many times it is quite evident their primary goal is not the gospel, but the pleasures of this life.

So, Mary, what happened to Ananias and Sapphira? Were these just special acts of God? Was God performing in such a way as to strike fear into the hearts of the Jews because He was favoring them and was contending to get them saved?

Will God perform for us Gentiles the same way He does for the Jews?

The answer is yes. He most emphatically will. This is why Peter said what he did in Acts 10:34-35:

> Then Peter opened his mouth, and said, Of a truth I perceive that God is no respecter of persons:
> 
> But in every nation he that feareth him, and worketh righteousness, is accepted with him.

So, Mary, it is impossible for God to be a respecter of persons. That means He will do everything He can to save the people of one generation as He will another. What God did in the book of Acts, He would do in a moment for our generation if He could find what He needed to do it with, because God is no respecter of persons.

I am not just talking about killing people, either. I am talking about the signs and wonders that confounded the book of Acts generation, and also the fullness of the mantle that was on the apostles, in particular Peter and later the apostle Paul. And I know there had to be many more just like them.

The mantle I am talking about is how God used them to speak judgment, in fact instantaneous judgment.

Just to jump ahead for a moment, the primary qualifying factor to speak this kind of judgment is the mercy that rejoices against judgment, as James 2:13 says:

> For he shall have judgment without mercy, that hath shewed no mercy; and mercy rejoiceth against judgment.

This is a primary qualification for judgment.

Last week we settled an issue after covering a couple of moves that were in fullness of mantle and manifestation in the early church, such as the fullness of the workings of miracles and gifts of healings, when they brought all the people who were demon possessed and sick from the cities round about.

So what constitutes this kind of judgment that He is talking about?

When the sinner thinks of judgment, he will remember part of one of the Ten Commandments. He will see a preacher or somebody doing something wrong, and then the preacher will tell him, "Look, you are going to go to hell."

And he will say, "Do not judge me. Do not judge me. Thou shalt not judge."

He will quote part of a verse.

But when Jesus says, "Judge not, lest you be judged," and then says the same measure you measure out in judging him will be measured back to you again, He continues by saying, "Why do you behold the splinter in your brother's eye when you have a whole beam in yours? First get the beam out of your eye, and then you can see to help your brother get the splinter out of his eye, you hypocrite."

What constitutes hypocrisy?

Hypocrisy is when you judge a brother for something you are guilty of. In the judgment He is talking about here, you hold him accountable and bring him under judgment for his fault, for what he is doing wrong to you or to other people, while you are doing exactly the same thing.

That is what constitutes hypocrisy, and that is what causes the judgment to come back on us.
